1.1 Risk Management: A Helicopter View

Learning Objectives

After completing this reading you should be able to:

  • Explain the concept of risk and compare risk management with risk taking.

  • Describe the risk management process and identify problems and challenges that can arise in the risk management process.

  • Evaluate and apply tools and procedures used to measure and manage risk, including quantitative measures, qualitative assessment, and enterprise risk management.

  • Distinguish between expected loss and unexpected loss, and provide examples of each.

  • Interpret the relationship between risk and reward and explain how conflicts of interest can impact risk management.

  • Describe and differentiate between the key classes of risks, explain how each type of risk can arise, and assess the potential impact of each type of risk on an organization.

LO 1.1: Explain the concept of risk and compare risk management with risk taking.

  • Risk arises from the uncertainty regarding an entity's future losses as well as future gains.

  • Risk management aims to reduce expected and unexpected loss.

  • Risk taking refers to incremental risk for incremental gains.

LO 1.2: Describe the risk management process and identify problems and challenges that can arise in the risk management process.

  • Risk must be sufficiently dispersed among willing and able participants in the economy.

  • Risk management can be thought of as a zero-sum game because it only involves risk transferring by one party and risk assumption by another party.

LO 1.3: Evaluate and apply tools and procedures used to measure and manage risk, including quantitative measures, qualitative assessment, and enterprise risk management.

  • Value at risk (VaR) states a certain loss amount and its probability of occurring. A VaR statement looks like: the involved entity having a minimum loss of $xxx during a xxx days is xxx%.

  • Economic capital refers to holding sufficient liquid reserves to cover a potential loss.

  • Scenario analysis takes into account potential risk factors with uncertainties that are often non-quantifiable: adverse scenario or worst-case scenario.

  • Stress testing is a form of scenario analysis that examines a financial outcome based on a given “stress” on the entity.

Notes: Scenario testing is akin to saying what if war break out in the Saudi Arabia ( for example ) how will variables change and it affect the model/portfolio. Stress testing is just moving the price of oil to various extremes to see the effect on the model/portfolio.

  • Enterprise Risk Management (ERM) takes an integrative approach to risk management within an entire entity, dispensing of the traditional approach of independently managing risk within each department or division of an entity.

LO 1.4: Distinguish between expected loss and unexpected loss, and provide examples of each.

  • Expected loss considers how much an entity expects to lose in the normal course of business.

  • Unexpected loss considers how much an entity could lose outside of the normal course of business.

  • Correlation risk, when unfavorable events happen together, drives up the potential losses to unexpected levels.

LO 1.5: Interpret the relationship between risk and reward and explain how conflicts of interest can impact risk management.

  • The greater the risk taken, the greater the potential reward.

  • The portion of the variability that is measurable as a probability function could be thought of as risk whereas the portion that is not measurable could be thought of as uncertainty.

LO 1.6: Describe and differentiate between the key classes of risks, explain how each type of risk can arise, and assess the potential impact of each type of risk on an organization.

There are eight key classes of risk: (1) market risk, (2) credit risk, (3) liquidity risk, (4) operational risk, (5) legal and regulatory risk, (6) business risk, (7) strategic risk, and (8) reputation risk.

  • Market risk considers how changes in market prices and rates will result in investment losses. There are four subtypes of market risk:

    • Interest rate risk

    • Equity price risk (general market risk + specific risk)

    • Foreign exchange risk

    • Commodity price risk

  • Credit risk refers to a party whereby the counterparty fails to meet its financial obligations to the party under the contract. There are four subtypes of credit risk:

    • Default risk (recovery value + loss given default, or LGD)

    • Bankruptcy risk

    • Downgrade risk

    • Settlement risk (Notes: Settlement risk is usually associated with derivatives contracts.)

  • Liquidity risk is subdivided into two parts:

    • Funding liquidity risk

    • Trading liquidity risk

  • Operational risk considers a wide range of “non-financial” problems such as inadequate computer systems (technology risk), insufficient internal controls, incompetent management, fraud, human error, and natural disasters.

  • Legal and regulatory risk

  • Business risk

  • Strategic risk

  • Reputation risk consists of two parts:

    • The general perceived trustworthiness of an entity.

    • The general perception that the entity engages in fair dealing and conducts business in an ethical manner.
